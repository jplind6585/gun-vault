Firearm Platforms & History

45 iconic firearms from 1630 to today — the guns that shaped history.

Flintlock Musket
1630–1840s · Europe
Musket
Dominant infantry arm for 200 years; won America's independence.
The flintlock ignition system — a flint striking a steel frizzen to produce a spark — replaced the matchlock and dominated infantry warfare from the 1630s through the Napoleonic Wars. Smoothbore, with effective range under 100 yards, armies compensated with massed volley fire. The Brown Bess (British) and Charleville (French) variants armed opposing sides in nearly every major 18th-century conflict including the American Revolution.
Percussion Cap Rifle
1820–1870s · Europe / USA
Musket
Chemical ignition replaced flint; first major reliability leap in infantry arms.
The percussion cap — a small copper cup containing fulminate of mercury — replaced the flintlock's unreliable flint-and-pan system. Misfires dropped dramatically. Combined with the Minié ball (1848), percussion rifles became accurate to 400+ yards, fundamentally changing Civil War tactics: smoothbore-era tactics met rifled-bore lethality, producing catastrophic frontal assault casualties at Fredericksburg, Cold Harbor, and elsewhere.
Colt Walker / Dragoon Revolver
1847–1873 · USA
Handgun
The most powerful handgun of the 19th century — designed for cavalry warfare.
Samuel Colt and Texas Ranger Samuel Walker co-designed the Walker Colt in 1847 to give mounted cavalry a weapon powerful enough to stop a horse. At 4.5 lbs unloaded, it was the largest and most powerful repeating handgun of its era — a 9-inch barrel firing a .44-caliber 220-grain ball over a massive powder charge. Only 1,100 were made, but they set the standard for Colt's percussion revolvers that followed, including the Dragoon and 1851 Navy. The Walker's black powder load produces muzzle energy comparable to modern .357 Magnum.
Henry Repeating Rifle
1860–1866 · USA
Lever Action
The original repeater — Confederate soldiers called it "that Yankee rifle you could load on Sunday and shoot all week."
Designed by Benjamin Tyler Henry, the 1860 Henry held 15 rounds of .44 rimfire — more than any military rifle of its era. Privately purchased by Union soldiers (the Army refused to adopt it, fearing men would waste ammunition), it gave individual infantrymen firepower that stunned Confederate forces. The Army's resistance was institutional: generals feared the logistical burden of a soldier who could fire 15 times without reloading. Henry's design became the direct predecessor to the Winchester 1866 and 1873.
Springfield Model 1861
1861–1865 · USA
Musket
The primary Union infantry rifle of the Civil War — over 1 million produced.
The Springfield M1861 was the most widely used rifle musket of the American Civil War. Chambered in .58 caliber with a Minié ball, it was accurate to 500 yards in trained hands. Its production in government and private arsenals exceeded 1 million units. The rifle's range and accuracy made Napoleonic massed infantry assaults suicidal — a lesson that took commanders years of bloody battles to learn.
The Carbine: Shorter, Lighter, Always Evolving
1860s–present · Europe / USA
Historical Context
From cavalry sabers to close-quarters combat — the fundamental problem has never had a permanent answer.
The carbine began as a cavalry necessity: a mounted soldier needed a shorter, lighter rifle he could manage on horseback. Civil War cavalry carbines (Spencer, Sharps, Burnside) were shorter and faster than infantry rifles — cavalry were actually better-armed per man than infantry by 1864. The concept evolved: WWII's M1 Carbine was designed for troops who needed more than a pistol but less than a full rifle — staff officers, vehicle crews, paratroopers. Today 'carbine' means a short-barreled variant like the M4 (14.5" vs the M16's 20"). The fundamental question — some soldiers need more than a handgun but less rifle than a rifleman — has driven weapons development across 160 years without a permanent resolution.
Colt Single Action Army
1873–present · USA
Handgun
The gun that won the West — standard US Army sidearm for 20 years.
Chambered in .45 Colt, the SAA was adopted by the US Army in 1873 and became the defining firearm of the American West. Its robust single-action mechanism and flat trajectory made it effective to 75 yards. Used by both sides in the Lincoln County War, carried by lawmen and outlaws alike. The Peacemaker, as it was called, remains in production today — one of the longest-running firearm designs in history.
Winchester Model 1873
1873–present · USA
Lever Action
"The gun that won the West" — same caliber as the Colt SAA for convenience.
Produced in pistol calibers (.44-40, .38-40, .32-20) to allow cowboys to carry a single cartridge type for both rifle and revolver, the Model 1873 was the most popular lever-action repeater of its era. Over 720,000 produced before 1919. The interchangeability with Colt revolvers was a deliberate commercial and practical decision. Still produced by Winchester and Uberti today as a tribute to its cultural significance.
Maxim Gun / Vickers
1884–1960s · UK
Machine Gun
First fully automatic firearm — redefined warfare and colonialism.
Hiram Maxim's recoil-operated machine gun fired 500 rounds per minute and changed the nature of warfare irreversibly. Water-cooled and belt-fed, it could hold off hundreds of infantry with a crew of two. At the Battle of Omdurman (1898), a handful of Maxim guns killed 10,000 Sudanese in under 5 hours. The Vickers variant served both World Wars and remained in British service until 1968. "Whatever happens, we have got / The Maxim gun, and they have not." — Hilaire Belloc
Mosin-Nagant M91/30
1891–1960s · Russia / USSR
Bolt Rifle
37 million produced — the most manufactured military rifle in history.
Designed jointly by Sergei Mosin and Leon Nagant, the 7.62x54mmR Mosin-Nagant was the standard Soviet infantry rifle through both World Wars and the Winter War. Over 37 million produced — one of the most manufactured rifles in history. The M91/30 PU sniper variant was used with devastating effect at Stalingrad and throughout the Eastern Front.
Winchester Model 1894 / 30-30
1894–present · USA
Lever Action
More North American deer have fallen to the 30-30 than any other cartridge — by a wide margin.
John Browning designed the Model 94 as the first lever-action built around smokeless powder, paired with the .30-30 Winchester — the first American sporting cartridge loaded with smokeless powder. Generations of hunters carried the 94 in the deer woods: light, fast-handling, reliable in cold and mud, accurate to 150 yards in iron-sight hands. Over 7 million produced. The 30-30's 150-grain flat-point bullet is not glamorous by modern standards, but it has cleanly taken more whitetail deer than any other cartridge in history. Still in production, still carried by guides in Canada's backcountry a century and a quarter after its introduction.
Lee-Enfield SMLE
1895–1957 (primary) · UK
Bolt Rifle
Fastest bolt action ever issued — trained British soldiers fired 15 rounds per minute.
The Short Magazine Lee-Enfield combined a 10-round magazine (double that of the Mauser) with a smooth cock-on-close bolt that allowed the legendary "mad minute" — 15 aimed rounds per minute demonstrated by trained British regulars. Over 17 million produced across its service life. The SMLE's rear-locking bolt is mechanically weaker than Mauser's front-locking design, but the speed advantage in combat was decisive. Still found in service in parts of South Asia today.
Mauser Gewehr 98 / K98k
1898–1945 (primary) · Germany
Bolt Rifle
The standard against which all bolt actions are measured — still copied today.
Paul Mauser's 1898 action set the template for virtually every sporting and military bolt action that followed. The dual-opposed-locking lug system, controlled-round feeding, and Mauser claw extractor are still considered the gold standard. The K98k (kurz, shortened) variant served as Germany's standard WWII rifle. Captured examples were prized by Allied soldiers. The action is directly licensed or copied in the Winchester Model 70, Remington 700, and dozens of other designs.
M1911 / 1911 Family
1911–present · USA
Handgun
74 years of continuous US military service — the longest-serving standard sidearm.
John Browning's .45 ACP semi-automatic pistol served as the US military's standard sidearm from 1911 to 1985 — 74 years of continuous service. The single-action locked-breech design became the template for modern semi-automatic pistols. Still in production by dozens of manufacturers and competitive in practical and bullseye shooting today.
Lewis Gun
1914–1950s · USA/UK
Machine Gun
WWI's squad automatic weapon — air-cooled and portable where the Maxim was not.
The Lewis Gun's pan magazine and air-cooled barrel made it the first truly portable automatic weapon at squad level. A single soldier could carry and operate it. Used extensively by British and Commonwealth forces in WWI, mounted on aircraft as one of the first air-to-air weapons, and carried into WWII and beyond. Its clock-spring pan magazine was an engineering compromise — slow to reload but it made the gun man-portable without a tripod crew.
Browning Automatic Rifle (BAR)
1918–1970s · USA
Machine Gun
Every WWII US squad's base of fire — John Browning's portable automatic.
The BAR M1918 gave US infantry squads automatic fire capability in a rifle-weight package. At 15.5 lbs, it was lighter than a machine gun but heavier than a rifle — a deliberate tradeoff. The 20-round .30-06 magazine limited sustained fire, but in the squad assault it provided the critical suppressive base. The BAR gunner was typically the first casualty enemy fire sought out. Clyde Barrow reportedly stole BARs from National Guard armories and preferred them over everything else he used.
Thompson Submachine Gun
1919–1971 · USA
Submachine Gun
The "Tommy Gun" — gangsters and GIs carried the same weapon.
John T. Thompson designed the "trench broom" to end WWI trench warfare — it arrived too late. Instead it became the weapon of choice for Prohibition-era gangsters, then US Marines in Nicaragua, then GIs storming Normandy. The .45 ACP cartridge hits hard at close range; the drum magazine is iconic. At 10+ lbs loaded, it was heavy, but the 1928 and M1 variants simplified it for mass wartime production. Over 1.7 million made during WWII.
M2 Browning .50 BMG
1933–present · USA
Machine Gun
Designed in 1918, still the standard heavy machine gun of NATO — 90+ years in service.
John Browning designed the M2 in response to General Pershing's request for a heavy machine gun after WWI. The .50 BMG (12.7×99mm) cartridge was developed alongside it. Originally designed to counter aircraft and light armor, the M2 has been mounted on aircraft, ships, tanks, helicopters, and ground vehicles. Its effective range against point targets exceeds 1,800 meters. The M2 has seen continuous production and service since 1933 — no replacement is in sight. Called "Ma Deuce" by generations of American soldiers.
NFA 1934: The Federal Government Regulates Firearms
1934–present · USA
Historical Context
Prohibition gangsters with Tommy Guns forced the first major federal gun law — it still governs machine guns and suppressors today.
The National Firearms Act of 1934 was a direct response to Prohibition-era violence. Thompson submachine guns and sawn-off shotguns had become symbols of organized crime; Congress responded by imposing a $200 transfer tax (equivalent to ~$4,500 today) and mandatory registration of machine guns, suppressors, short-barreled rifles, and shotguns. The tax was designed to be prohibitive. The 1986 Hughes Amendment closed the civilian machine gun registry — no new machine guns may be added — leaving about 182,000 registered transferable examples whose prices have since risen to $20,000–$50,000+. The NFA framework has not fundamentally changed in 90 years.
M1 Garand
1936–1957 (primary) · USA
Battle Rifle
First semi-automatic rifle adopted as a standard infantry weapon — General Patton called it the greatest battle implement ever devised.
John Garand's en-bloc clip-fed .30-06 semi-automatic rifle gave US infantry a firepower advantage over bolt-action-equipped enemies throughout WWII. Eight rounds, semi-automatic, clip ejects automatically when empty with a distinctive "ping." The ping became legend — supposedly alerting enemies — but in actual combat the noise of battle rendered it irrelevant. Over 5.4 million produced. The M1 also sparked the caliber debate: if a semi-auto rifle required so much ammunition, was .30-06 the right cartridge, or was something smaller and lighter better?
Boys Anti-Tank Rifle
1937–1945 · UK
Anti-Materiel
WWII's crew-portable tank killer — obsolete against panzers by 1941, lethal against everything else.
The Boys fired a .55-caliber (13.9mm) round capable of penetrating 20mm of armor at 100 yards — sufficient against early WWII light tanks, quickly outpaced by German armor. What the Boys couldn't kill through a tank's front plate it could kill through engine blocks, aircraft fuselages, and field fortifications. Finnish forces used captured Boys rifles against Soviet light armor in the Winter War with notable effect. Its brutal recoil impulse was legendary — soldiers dreaded firing it prone on frozen ground. Over 62,000 produced.
The WWII Caliber Debate
1940–1955 · USA / UK / Germany
Historical Context
Full-power vs. intermediate cartridge — the argument that shaped every service rifle after 1945.
By 1940, infantry combat data showed most engagements occurred under 300 meters — well within the capability of a lighter cartridge than .30-06 or 7.92 Mauser. Germany's answer was the 7.92×33mm Kurz and the StG-44. The British experimented with .280 (7×43mm). The US Army, conservatively committed to long-range marksmanship doctrine, insisted on full-power cartridges. After WWII, NATO standardized on 7.62×51mm — a shortened but barely lighter .30-06 equivalent — over British objections. The Soviets chose the 7.62×39mm intermediate for the AK-47. The US eventually adopted 5.56×45mm in 1963. The intermediate cartridge won, but it took 20 years of institutional resistance.
PTRD-41 / PTRS-41
1941–1950s · USSR
Anti-Materiel
Designed and mass-produced in months after Barbarossa — the Red Army's desperate anti-armor answer.
When German panzers invaded in 1941, the Red Army had virtually no portable anti-tank capability. The PTRD (single-shot bolt-action) and PTRS (semi-automatic) were designed and rushed into mass production under wartime duress. The 14.5×114mm cartridge — the most powerful round in widespread military use — could defeat German side and rear armor through mid-war. Pairs of anti-tank riflemen worked from concealed positions, firing and immediately relocating. The cartridge outlasted the rifle, living on in the KPV heavy machine gun and still in service today.
StG 44 (Sturmgewehr)
1944–1945 · Germany
Assault Rifle
The first assault rifle — its concept outlived the regime that built it.
The Sturmgewehr 44 combined a 30-round detachable magazine, selective fire (semi and full auto), and an intermediate 7.92×33mm Kurz cartridge into the first weapon we would recognize as an assault rifle. Hitler reportedly named it himself (overruling the "MP43" designation to hide its development from him). Late-war Wehrmacht units equipped with it outperformed those with bolt actions. Its concept — intermediate cartridge, high-capacity magazine, selective fire — directly influenced Kalashnikov's AK-47 design.
WWII Surplus and the Civilian Gun Market
1945–1968 · USA
Historical Context
Returning GIs brought military gun culture home — and a mail-order Carcano ended the era.
The US demobilized 12 million men after WWII, many of whom had fired M1 Garands and 1911s daily for years. Surplus firearms flooded the civilian market through the 1950s–60s, available by mail order from magazine ads. The Civilian Marksmanship Program sold M1 Garands for $7.50 each. European surplus — Mausers, Mosin-Nagants, Lee-Enfields — arrived by the crate. This era built a generation of American gun owners with direct military connections to their firearms. The assassination of President Kennedy with a $12.78 mail-order Carcano rifle in 1963 directly produced the Gun Control Act of 1968, which ended mail-order firearm sales and required federal dealer licenses.
AK-47 / AKM / AK-74 Family
1947–present · USSR / Russia
Assault Rifle
75–100 million produced — the most manufactured firearm in human history.
Mikhail Kalashnikov's gas-operated rifle emerged from WWII combat experience. The long-stroke piston system and loose tolerances give it legendary reliability in adverse conditions. An estimated 75–100 million AK-pattern rifles have been produced — the most manufactured firearm in history.
Marlin 336
1948–present · USA
Lever Action
Winchester's chief rival — side-ejection lets you mount a scope where the 94 cannot.
Marlin's answer to the Winchester 94, the 336 uses a side-ejection system rather than top-ejection — which allows scope mounting directly above the bore, a critical advantage as glass sights replaced iron during the post-WWII era. In .30-30 and later .35 Remington, it found a devoted following among hunters who wanted the handling of a lever gun with the optics compatibility of a bolt action. The 336 and 94 have competed for the American hunting market for over 70 years, each loyal to a different generation.
FN FAL
1953–present · Belgium
Assault Rifle
"The right arm of the free world" — adopted by over 90 countries during the Cold War.
FN Herstal's Fusil Automatique Léger in 7.62×51mm NATO was adopted by more Western nations during the Cold War than any other rifle. Its tilting-bolt gas-operated action is reliable across climates. The UK's L1A1 SLR version (semi-auto only) served through the Falklands War. Argentina and the UK both used FALs against each other in 1982 — the only time the same weapon has faced itself in a major conflict. Israel, Australia, Canada, and dozens of African and South American militaries issued it.
S&W Model 29 (.44 Magnum)
1955–present · USA
Handgun
"The most powerful handgun in the world" — built because Elmer Keith refused to stop pushing the limits.
For decades, wildcatter and hunter Elmer Keith had been loading .44 Special cases to pressures their frames were never designed for, producing a cartridge he knew could stop any North American game. He badgered Smith & Wesson and Remington until they commercialized it: the .44 Remington Magnum in the S&W Model 29 N-frame revolver. Introduced in 1955, it was the world's most powerful production handgun. Dirty Harry made it famous in 1971 — 'the most powerful handgun in the world' — and backordered the gun for years. The Model 29 remains the defining large-bore revolver.
Elmer Keith: The Man Who Made Magnums
1930s–1970s · USA
Historical Context
An Idaho rancher with a handloading press and no fear of overpressure — he forced the industry to build the magnums.
Elmer Keith was an Idaho rancher, hunting guide, and writer who spent decades loading handgun cartridges to pressures their manufacturers expressly warned against — and publishing the results in Guns & Ammo and American Rifleman. He believed factory loads were inadequate for large game. By repeatedly demonstrating his wildcat loads, he forced industry action: the .357 Magnum (1934), co-developed with S&W and Winchester, extended the .38 Special case and proved his point. He did the same for the .44 Special, badgering S&W and Remington for two decades until the .44 Magnum appeared in 1955. Later, the .41 Magnum (1964). Keith also wrote the definitive texts on handgun hunting and shooting. He is the single greatest reason modern large-bore revolvers exist.
M14 Rifle
1957–1970 (primary) · USA
Battle Rifle
NATO's answer to the AK-47 — too heavy, too long, almost immediately replaced.
The M14 was the US Army's post-WWII modernization: a select-fire, detachable-magazine 7.62×51mm NATO rifle intended to replace the M1 Garand, BAR, M1 Carbine, and M3 submachine gun in one design. It failed at all of them. In full-auto the recoil was uncontrollable; the wooden stock warped in jungle humidity; the length was awkward in close quarters. Adopted in 1957, it was being phased out before 1970. Modernized as the M21 and M14 EBR, it serves in a sniper/designated marksman role where its 7.62 range matters more than its weight.
AR-15 / M16 / M4 Family
1959–present · USA
Assault Rifle
The most-produced rifle in American history — in service every year since 1959.
Designed by Eugene Stoner for ArmaLite, the AR-15 is the most-produced rifle platform in American history. The direct-impingement gas system and modular lower/upper split have spawned an entire ecosystem of parts, calibers, and configurations. The M16 and M4 variants have served in every US conflict since Vietnam.
Remington Model 700
1962–present · USA
Bolt Rifle
Foundation of the US military sniper program — M24 and M40 are both 700 variants.
The most successful bolt-action rifle in American history. The Model 700's push-feed action and aftermarket ecosystem made it the foundation for US military and law enforcement sniper programs (M24, M40). Chambered in over 40 calibers over its production run.
HK MP5
1966–present · Germany
Submachine Gun
Defined hostage rescue for 50 years — still preferred over SBRs in many police roles.
Heckler & Koch's roller-delayed blowback SMG became the definitive hostage rescue weapon after its use by GSG-9 at Mogadishu in 1977. Over 30 variants produced. Renowned for accuracy relative to other SMGs. Still preferred in many law enforcement roles despite being replaced by SBRs in military use.
SIG Sauer P226 / P-Series
1980–present · Switzerland/Germany
Handgun
Lost the Army trials on price, not performance — the SEALs adopted it anyway.
The P226 narrowly lost the 1984 US Army trials to the Beretta 92 on cost — not performance. The Navy SEALs, unwilling to accept that verdict, adopted it anyway where it served for decades. The DA/SA trigger and all-metal construction give it a reputation for smoothness and durability that spawned the P229, P228, and modern P320.
Barrett M82 / M107
1982–present · USA
Anti-Materiel
Semi-automatic .50 BMG — engages targets at ranges that redefined "sniper."
Ronnie Barrett's semi-automatic .50 BMG rifle entered military service in Desert Storm, used to defeat light armor and explosive ordnance at extreme ranges. The recoil-operated action and muzzle brake make the massive cartridge manageable. Maximum effective range against point targets exceeds 1,800 meters.
Glock 17 / Glock Family
1982–present · Austria
Handgun
Carried by 65% of US law enforcement — the polymer pistol template every competitor copies.
Gaston Glock's polymer-frame, striker-fired pistol revolutionized the handgun industry when the Austrian Army adopted it in 1982. The safe-action trigger, high-capacity magazines, and simple field-strip set the template for virtually every modern polymer pistol. Carried by over 65% of US law enforcement agencies.
Beretta M9 / 92FS
1985–2017 (US military) · Italy
Handgun
Beat the SIG P226 on price — served 32 years before the P320 replaced it.
In 1985, the US military conducted trials to replace the M1911. The Beretta 92FS beat the SIG P226 by meeting all requirements at lower cost. The 9mm switch from .45 ACP was controversial — some argued for stopping power, others for magazine capacity and NATO standardization. The Beretta served through Desert Storm, Iraq, and Afghanistan. A notable issue: the locking block could fracture on high-pressure ammunition, causing the slide to detach. Modified versions addressed this. Replaced by the SIG P320 (M17/M18) in 2017.
Shall-Issue CCW: The Concealed Carry Revolution
1987–present · USA
Historical Context
Florida's 1987 law began a 35-year march — today 29 states require no permit at all.
Until 1987, most US states were 'may-issue' — police chiefs decided who received carry permits, and in most urban areas they declined almost everyone. Florida's landmark 1987 shall-issue law changed the model: meet objective criteria (background check, training), and the state shall issue the permit. Criminologists predicted blood in the streets; the feared spike in violence did not materialize. By 2000, 33 states were shall-issue. By 2022, a Supreme Court ruling (NYSRPA v. Bruen) struck down may-issue as unconstitutional. Today, 29 states are 'constitutional carry' — no permit required. Approximately 22 million Americans hold carry permits; the number carrying without a permit in permissive states is unknown but likely larger.
The 1994 Assault Weapons Ban
1994–2004 · USA
Historical Context
Ten years of "no flash hiders, no pistol grips" — a ban that reshaped the market and expired by design.
The Federal Assault Weapons Ban (1994–2004) prohibited manufacture of semi-automatic rifles with more than one 'evil feature': pistol grip, folding stock, flash hider, bayonet lug, or threaded barrel. Magazines over 10 rounds were also banned for new manufacture. The result: manufacturers produced 'featureless' AR-15s with thumbhole stocks and sold them legally. Pre-ban magazines commanded premiums. When the ban sunset in September 2004 — Congress had written the expiration in, and declined to renew it — sales of standard-capacity magazines and feature-complete ARs surged immediately. The ban is widely credited with building the political coalition that blocked its renewal and accelerating the civilian AR market.
FN SCAR (Mk 16 / Mk 17)
2004–present · Belgium
Assault Rifle
SOF's precision rifle — the SCAR-H is the preferred DMR for many special operations units.
FN Herstal's Special Operations Forces Combat Assault Rifle won the SOCOM SCAR competition in 2004. Available in 5.56mm (Mk 16) and 7.62mm (Mk 17) variants. The SCAR-H has become the preferred precision/designated marksman platform for many SOF units worldwide.
HK416
2004–present · Germany
Assault Rifle
The AR-15 redesigned with a piston system — used by Delta Force, SEAL Team 6, and French Army.
Designed at the request of Delta Force to address M4 reliability issues in sandy environments, the HK416 replaces the AR-15's direct-impingement gas system with a short-stroke piston — keeping propellant gases out of the receiver. The action runs cooler and cleaner. It was the weapon used in the bin Laden raid. Norway adopted it as the HK416N; France replaced the FAMAS with it as the primary French military rifle. The 10.4" variant in .300 Blackout is widely used by special operations forces.
The Civilian AR-15: America's Most Popular Rifle
2004–present · USA
Historical Context
From niche military enthusiast to 20 million in circulation — the AWB expiration changed everything.
Before the 1994 AWB expired, civilian AR-15 ownership was a niche interest — veterans, competitors, enthusiasts. After 2004, the market exploded. Manufacturers flooded retail; prices dropped; the modular platform attracted a new generation of builders and customizers. Demand spiked after the 2008 and 2012 elections on anticipated bans that never came. The AR-15 became simultaneously the most discussed, most feared, and most purchased rifle in American history. Estimates put civilian AR-pattern rifles in circulation at 20–25 million by 2024. It has spawned an ecosystem of accessories, competitive shooting sports, custom shops, and sustained political debate. It is America's most popular rifle by every metric.
SIG M17 / M18 (P320)
2017–present · USA/Germany
Handgun
Won the MHS competition — then faced allegations of dropping when dropped.
The SIG Sauer P320 won the US Army's Modular Handgun System competition in 2017, replacing the Beretta M9 after 32 years. The M17 (full-size) and M18 (compact) share a serialized fire control unit that can be swapped between frame sizes — a genuinely novel approach. Shortly after adoption, reports emerged of the P320 firing when dropped at specific angles without the trigger being pulled. SIG contested the characterizations but issued a voluntary upgrade. The military versions include a manual safety and modified trigger geometry. It remains the current US military sidearm.
SIG MCX SPEAR / XM7
2022–present · USA
Assault Rifle
Next Generation Squad Weapon — designed to defeat modern body armor that 5.56 cannot.
The US Army's Next Generation Squad Weapon program concluded that 5.56×45mm is insufficient against current and projected enemy body armor at combat distances. The XM7 (rifle) and XM250 (automatic rifle) fire 6.8×51mm Common Cartridge — a hybrid case with a steel head capable of 80,000 PSI chamber pressure. The SIG MCX SPEAR action was redesigned to handle these pressures. Critics note the ammunition is heavy (reducing soldier load capacity), the weapon is heavier than the M4, and the logistics tail is enormous. Proponents argue it defeats any currently fielded body armor at 600 meters. Full-rate production is underway as of 2024.
