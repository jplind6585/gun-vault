Firearms Glossary

76 terms, acronyms, and definitions for shooters of every level.

+P
Overpressure Ammunition
Ammunition loaded to higher pressure than the standard SAAMI specification. Marked +P. Produces higher velocity but accelerates wear. Only use in firearms rated for +P.
+P+
Over-Overpressure Ammunition
Loaded even higher than +P. No SAAMI standard exists for this designation; it is manufacturer-defined. Treat as non-standard and verify firearm compatibility before use.
Action
Operating Mechanism
The mechanical system that loads, fires, and ejects cartridges. Common types: Semi-Automatic (auto-loads next round after each shot), Bolt-Action (manually cycle a bolt handle between shots), Lever-Action (work a lever under the receiver), Revolver (rotating cylinder), Pump (slide a fore-end), Single-Shot (one round at a time).
ACU
Army Combat Uniform
The US Army uniform platform that replaced BDUs in 2004, initially paired with the UCP pattern.
AIWB
Appendix Inside the Waistband
IWB holster worn at the 12 o'clock (front) position. Allows fast draw but requires discipline and quality holster.
AOW
Any Other Weapon
NFA catch-all category for concealable firearms that don't fit other regulated categories. Includes pen guns, cane guns, and some pistol-gripped shotguns.
AP
Armor Piercing
Hardened core (typically tungsten or steel) for penetrating armor. Heavily regulated under the Gun Control Act; most rifle AP and all handgun AP is banned for civilian sale.
APCR
Armor Piercing Composite Rigid
WWII-era anti-armor round featuring a tungsten carbide core inside a lighter carrier body. Higher velocity than conventional AP but less effective at oblique angles.
API
Armor Piercing Incendiary
AP round with an incendiary compound behind the penetrator. Ignites on impact. Largely prohibited for civilian use.
Barrel Length
Barrel Length
Distance from the chamber to the muzzle, measured in inches. Longer barrels produce higher velocity and are easier to aim accurately at distance. Shorter barrels are more compact. Legally significant: rifles must have a barrel of 16"+ and shotguns 18"+ to avoid NFA regulation.
BC
Ballistic Coefficient
A number describing how efficiently a bullet resists air drag. Higher BC = less drop and wind drift at distance. G1 and G7 are the two common drag models.
BCG
Bolt Carrier Group
The reciprocating assembly in an AR-pattern rifle: bolt, carrier, firing pin, cam pin, and retaining pin. The heart of the gas-operated action.
BDC
Bullet Drop Compensator
Reticle subtensions or a turret cam designed to compensate for bullet drop at set distances. Only accurate for the specific ammo the scope was calibrated with.
Bolt-Action
Bolt-Action Firearm
A firearm type where the shooter manually lifts and pulls back a bolt handle to eject the fired case, then pushes it forward to chamber a new round. Simple, reliable, and extremely accurate. The standard choice for hunting rifles and precision long-range shooting.
Caliber
Cartridge / Bore Diameter
The size designation of a firearm and the ammunition it fires — usually the bullet's diameter in inches or millimeters (e.g., 9mm, .45 ACP, .308 Win). A firearm can only safely fire the specific caliber it is chambered for.
Capacity
Magazine or Cylinder Capacity
The maximum number of rounds a magazine (semi-automatic) or cylinder (revolver) can hold before requiring a reload. May be legally restricted to 10 rounds or fewer in some states.
CCW
Concealed Carry Weapon
A firearm carried in a concealed manner on the person. Also refers to the permit itself in states that require one.
CEP
Circular Error Probable
Radius of a circle within which 50% of shots will land. A standard military accuracy metric; smaller CEP = more accurate system.
Chamber
Cartridge Chamber
The cavity at the rear of the barrel where a cartridge sits when ready to fire. "Chambered" means a round is loaded and ready. "Clearing the chamber" means verifying it is empty — a core safety step.
CIP
Commission Internationale Permanente
The European standards body for cartridge dimensions and pressures, equivalent to SAAMI. Most European and many Asian firearms are CIP-proofed.
DA
Double Action
Trigger mechanism where pulling the trigger both cocks and releases the hammer or striker. Longer, heavier pull than SA but can fire from a decocked state.
DA/SA
Double Action / Single Action
Hybrid system: first shot fires DA (long heavy pull from decocked), subsequent shots fire SA (short light pull with hammer cocked). Common in Beretta, SIG, and CZ designs.
DAO
Double Action Only
Always fires in double action; no external hammer to cock manually. Consistent trigger pull for every shot. Common in DAO revolvers and some compact semi-autos.
DOPE
Data On Previous Engagements
A shooter's recorded scope adjustments (elevation and windage) for a specific rifle, ammunition, and distance combination. The foundation of precision long-range shooting.
Double Feed
Double Feed Malfunction
Two rounds attempting to enter the chamber simultaneously. Typically caused by a failure to extract. Requires an emergency reload procedure: lock bolt back, strip magazine, rack three times, reload.
EDC
Every Day Carry
A firearm (or gear loadout) that a person carries daily for personal protection. EDC philosophy emphasizes balancing concealability with capability.
Elevation
Vertical Scope / Sight Adjustment
The up/down adjustment on a scope or iron sight. Dialing elevation up raises the bullet's point of impact. Used to compensate for bullet drop at longer distances.
ES
Extreme Spread
The difference between the fastest and slowest velocity in a string of shots, measured by chronograph. Also used to describe the largest distance between any two shots in a group.
FFL
Federal Firearms License
Required to commercially manufacture, import, or deal in firearms in the United States. Multiple license types exist; Type 01 is the common dealer FFL.
FFP
First Focal Plane
Scope reticle mounted in the front focal plane; scales with magnification. MOA/MIL holds and measurements are correct at any power setting. Preferred for variable-power precision scopes.
FMJ
Full Metal Jacket
Bullet with a soft lead core fully enclosed in a harder metal jacket (usually gilding metal or copper-washed steel). Does not expand. Standard practice and range ammunition; required by Hague Convention for military use.
FTE
Failure to Eject
A fired case that fails to leave the action after extraction. Often caused by a weak or dirty extractor, or low-pressure ammunition in a gas-operated system.
FTF
Failure to Feed
A malfunction where a round fails to chamber from the magazine. Causes include damaged magazine lips, weak recoil spring, or improper ammunition seating.
FTFire
Failure to Fire
The primer was struck by the firing pin but the round did not fire. Causes: light primer strike, hard primer, wet or degraded powder, or a squib condition. Wait 30 seconds before opening the action.
G1/G7
Ballistic Drag Function Models
Reference projectile shapes used to calculate BC. G1 (flat-base blunt nose) is traditional but less accurate for long-range. G7 (boat-tail spitzer) is more accurate for modern boat-tail rifle bullets beyond 400 yards.
Grain (gr)
Grain — Unit of Bullet Weight
The standard unit for measuring bullet and powder weight. 1 grain = 1/7000th of a pound. Common pistol bullets are 115–230 gr; rifle bullets 55–175 gr. Heavier bullets are slower but carry more energy at distance; lighter bullets are faster and shoot flatter.
HPBT
Hollow Point Boat Tail
A boat-tail bullet with a small open tip formed by the jacketing process. Functionally identical to OTM; the open tip improves manufacturing uniformity, not expansion. The standard precision match bullet form.
IWB
Inside the Waistband
A holster style worn inside the waistband, between the body and pants. Provides better concealment than OWB but requires slightly larger clothing.
JHP
Jacketed Hollow Point
A jacketed bullet with a hollow cavity in the tip that causes controlled expansion on impact. Transfers more energy to the target and reduces over-penetration. The standard defensive ammunition choice.
JSP
Jacketed Soft Point
A partial-jacket bullet with an exposed lead tip that promotes controlled expansion. Better feeding in semi-automatic actions than a plain soft point. Common in hunting and some defensive loads.
LRBHO
Last Round Bolt Hold Open
A feature that automatically locks the bolt back when the magazine is empty. Signals the shooter to reload and speeds the reload process. Standard on most modern semi-automatic pistols and rifles.
Magazine
Detachable Magazine
A spring-loaded container that holds and feeds cartridges into a semi-automatic firearm. Not a "clip" — magazines are self-contained feeding devices. Revolvers use a rotating cylinder instead. Magazines are typically made of metal or polymer.
MEP
Mean Point of Impact
The statistical center point of a shot group — the average X/Y coordinate of all impacts. Distinct from the tightest cluster; used when evaluating zero and ammo consistency.
MIL / MRAD
Milliradian
An angular unit of measure. 1 MIL = 3.6 inches at 100 yards (10cm at 100m). Common in military and precision rifle optics. A MIL-based system allows direct range estimation and holdover calculation without unit conversion.
MOA
Minute of Angle
An angular unit equal to 1/60th of a degree, or approximately 1.047 inches at 100 yards. Used to measure group size and to specify scope adjustment. Most scopes click in 1/4 MOA or 1/8 MOA increments.
MTP
Multi-Terrain Pattern
British armed forces camouflage pattern developed by Crye Precision, replacing DPM. A modified MultiCam tuned for British requirements. Adopted in 2010 after Afghanistan combat experience.
Muzzle
Muzzle — Front of the Barrel
The open front end of the barrel where the bullet exits. Rule #2 of firearm safety: never point the muzzle at anything you are not willing to destroy. Muzzle devices (suppressors, flash hiders, muzzle brakes) thread onto the muzzle.
Muzzle Velocity
Bullet Speed at the Muzzle (fps)
The speed of a bullet as it exits the barrel, measured in feet per second (fps). Higher velocity = flatter trajectory and more energy delivered to the target. Affected by barrel length, powder charge, bullet weight, and temperature.
NATO
North Atlantic Treaty Organization
NATO-spec ammunition meets STANAG standardization agreements for pressure, dimensions, and interchangeability. NATO 5.56mm runs at higher pressure than SAAMI .223 Rem; use caution in .223-only chambers.
NFA
National Firearms Act (1934)
Federal law regulating machine guns, suppressors, short-barreled rifles, short-barreled shotguns, AOWs, and destructive devices. Requires registration, $200 tax stamp (for most items), and ATF approval.
OC
Open Carry
Carrying a firearm in a visible, unconcealed manner. Legality varies widely by state and jurisdiction. Some states are permissive; others require a permit even for open carry.
OTM
Open Tip Match
A boat-tail bullet with a small opening at the tip formed by drawing the jacket from the base. The open tip is an artifact of manufacturing, not designed for expansion. Preferred for its manufacturing consistency in precision shooting.
OWB
Outside the Waistband
A holster worn on the outside of the belt. Easier to draw from and more comfortable than IWB, but requires a cover garment for concealment.
Par Time
Par Time — Training Benchmark
The target completion time for a shooting drill, measured with a shot timer. Finishing under par = pass; over par = more practice needed. A structured way to track improvement over time without just "shooting for fun."
Plinking
Casual Informal Shooting
Informal, recreational shooting at cans, bottles, or paper targets — just for fun, no training structure or scoring. Great way for new shooters to build comfort and confidence with a firearm.
POA
Point of Aim
Where the sights or reticle are directed. The difference between POA and POI is the offset the shooter must account for at any given range.
POI
Point of Impact
Where the bullet actually strikes. A properly zeroed rifle has POA = POI at the zero distance; the two diverge at other distances due to bullet drop and wind drift.
Reticle
Scope Crosshair / Aiming Pattern
The aiming pattern visible inside a scope or reflex sight. Can be a simple crosshair (+), dot, mil-dot grid, or complex BDC pattern. Illuminated reticles glow for low-light use. The reticle is what you align on the target before pressing the trigger.
Round Count
Total Rounds Fired
The cumulative number of rounds (bullets) fired through a specific firearm. Tracked to monitor wear, plan maintenance intervals, and determine when components like recoil springs should be replaced. Similar to mileage on a car.
SA
Single Action
Trigger mechanism that only releases the hammer or striker; the action must be pre-cocked externally. Short, light trigger pull. Standard on 1911s, revolvers in SA mode, and many competition pistols.
SAAMI
Sporting Arms and Ammunition Manufacturers' Institute
The US standards body that sets specifications for cartridge dimensions, pressures, and performance. If a cartridge is SAAMI-spec, any SAAMI-spec firearm in that caliber should safely chamber it.
SAO
Single Action Only
Firearm that can only fire in single action — the striker or hammer must be cocked before firing. Requires manual cocking or a cycling action to prepare. Common on 1911-pattern pistols.
SBR
Short Barreled Rifle
A rifle with a barrel under 16" or overall length under 26". An NFA item in the United States; requires a $200 tax stamp and ATF approval. Penalties for unregistered SBRs are severe.
SBS
Short Barreled Shotgun
A shotgun with a barrel under 18" or overall length under 26". NFA item, same registration process as SBRs.
SD
Sectional Density
Bullet weight (in pounds) divided by the square of its diameter (in inches). Higher SD = better penetration for a given bullet construction. A key metric for hunting and barrier penetration.
SD (velocity)
Standard Deviation of Velocity
A statistical measure of velocity consistency across a string of shots. Lower is better. Under 10 fps is excellent; over 20 fps suggests a handloading or quality issue affecting precision at distance.
Semi-Automatic
Semi-Automatic Action
A firearm that automatically ejects the spent case and chambers the next round after each shot — but requires a separate trigger pull for each bullet fired. Not "fully automatic" (which fires continuously while the trigger is held). The most common action in modern pistols, many rifles, and some shotguns.
SFP
Second Focal Plane
Scope reticle mounted behind the erector assembly; stays the same apparent size regardless of magnification. MOA/MIL values on the reticle are only correct at one specific power (usually max). Common in budget and medium-tier scopes.
SOT
Special Occupational Taxpayer
A Federal Firearms Licensee who has paid the Special Occupational Tax to deal in, manufacture, or import NFA items as a business. SOTs can transfer NFA items on a dealer sample basis without the standard $200 tax.
SP
Soft Point
A bullet with an exposed lead tip and partial metal jacket. The exposed lead promotes controlled expansion on impact. A traditional hunting bullet design, effective across a wide range of game.
Stovepipe
Stovepipe Malfunction (FTE Variant)
A failure to eject where the empty case stands upright in the ejection port, resembling a stovepipe. Cleared with a tap-rack or a swipe of the firing hand. Often caused by a limp wrist grip or dirty extractor.
Suppressor
Sound Suppressor / Silencer
A device threaded onto the muzzle that reduces the sound of a gunshot by trapping and cooling expanding gases. Often called a "silencer" — but real suppressors reduce, not eliminate, the sound. Regulated as NFA items in the US: $200 tax stamp, ATF approval, months-long wait.
TMJ
Total Metal Jacket
A bullet fully encased in metal jacket including the base, unlike standard FMJ. Eliminates base exposure that vaporizes lead under high heat. Preferred for indoor ranges to reduce airborne lead contamination.
Turret
Scope Adjustment Turret
The knobs on the top and side of a scope used to adjust point of impact. The elevation turret adjusts up/down; the windage turret adjusts left/right. Each click moves impact by a specific MOA or MIL increment. "Capping the turrets" protects them from accidental adjustment.
Windage
Horizontal Scope / Sight Adjustment
The left/right adjustment on a scope or iron sight, used to correct for wind drift or zero error. Turning windage right moves the point of impact right. Paired with elevation (up/down), windage completes the two-axis adjustment system of all modern optics.
Zero / Zeroing
Sight Alignment at a Specific Distance
The process of adjusting a scope or iron sights so the point of aim (where the crosshair points) matches the point of impact (where the bullet lands) at a specific distance. A "100-yard zero" means the gun is sighted in to hit exactly where you aim at 100 yards. Bullets drop beyond that distance due to gravity.
